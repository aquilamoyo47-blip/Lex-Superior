# Formal Legal Writing — Advocacy Guides for Zimbabwe Superior Courts
## Founding Affidavits, Heads of Argument, and Pleadings Style

### Part 1: The Art of Founding Affidavits

**Purpose of the Founding Affidavit**

The founding affidavit is the cornerstone of motion proceedings. It must contain every fact necessary to support the relief sought. Because in motion proceedings evidence is primarily on affidavit, the founding affidavit bears the full burden of establishing the applicant's case.

*Key principles:*
1. The founding affidavit must be complete. Facts not in the founding affidavit cannot ordinarily be introduced later (save in exceptional circumstances under a supplementary affidavit with leave of court).
2. All material facts must be positively stated.
3. Information and belief must be clearly identified as such.
4. Documents referred to should be attached as annexures.
5. The deponent must be a person with direct knowledge of the facts.

**Structure of the Founding Affidavit**

Para 1-3: Identification of deponent and authority to depose
Para 4-5: Background and context
Para 6 onwards: Substantive facts supporting the relief
Second-last paragraph: Statement of law and grounds for relief
Last paragraph: Prayer (what the court is asked to do)

**Common Errors**

(a) *Argument in the affidavit* — affidavits should contain facts, not argument. Legal submissions belong in heads of argument.
(b) *Hearsay without proper attribution* — "I am advised that..." without identifying the source is impermissible.
(c) *Conclusionary statements* — "the respondent acted unlawfully" is a conclusion; "the respondent failed to comply with section 14 of the Act" is a fact.
(d) *Annexures not identified* — annexures must be identified by mark in the body of the affidavit (e.g., "Annexure A") and attached.

### Part 2: Heads of Argument — Advanced Advocacy

**Purpose**

Heads of argument are the written submissions of counsel. They guide the court to the relevant law and its application to the facts. Good heads of argument—
(a) identify the issues concisely;
(b) state the law clearly;
(c) apply the law to the specific facts;
(d) anticipate and address the opponent's likely arguments;
(e) conclude with the order sought.

**Opening the Heads**

The introduction should orient the court—
"This is an urgent application for a prohibitory interdict restraining the Respondent from acting in contravention of Order 4 of the existing consent order dated [DATE]. The Applicant is entitled to the interdict on all three requirements: a clear right, a reasonable apprehension of harm, and no adequate alternative remedy."

This tells the court immediately: what kind of application, what is sought, and why it will succeed.

**Dealing with the Facts**

Do not re-narrate the affidavit. Summarise only the material facts, cross-referenced to the record. Use paragraph numbers from the affidavit.

"The Applicant was employed as a manager from January 2020 (FA para 5). His employment contract contains a restraint of trade clause (Annexure A, clause 12). On 15 March 2025, the Respondent wrongfully terminated his employment (FA para 18-22)."

**Legal Argument**

State each legal proposition in a separate numbered paragraph. Support it with authority. Apply it to the facts.

"15. For a final interdict, the Applicant must establish: (a) a clear right; (b) injury actually committed or reasonably apprehended; and (c) the absence of an adequate alternative remedy (Setlogelo v Setlogelo 1914 AD 221, applied in Zimbabwe in Airfield Investments (Pvt) Ltd v Minister of Lands 2004 (2) ZLR 433 (H)).

16. First requirement — clear right: The Applicant's right under the contract of employment is undisputed (Annexure A). The right is clear.

17. Second requirement — injury: The Respondent's letter of dismissal (Annexure B) constitutes an injury; the threatened further unlawful acts constitute a reasonable apprehension of further injury.

18. Third requirement — no adequate alternative remedy: A damages claim is not adequate because [reason]; and/or the harm is irreparable because [reason]."

**Costs**

Always address costs, citing the appropriate basis. The general rule is that costs follow the event, per Rule 123 of the High Court Rules. Special orders of costs (on the attorney-client scale) are available where a party has conducted litigation vexatiously or in bad faith.

### Part 3: Oral Advocacy

**Opening Address**

In the High Court, counsel opens by stating the nature of the matter:
"My Lord, this is an opposed application for summary judgment. The Applicant claims payment of USD 50,000.00 being money loaned and advanced. The Respondent has raised a purported defence of payment, which is not bona fide."

**Addressing the Court on the Law**

Lead with your strongest point. Do not bury key submissions. Signal to the court when you are moving between issues.

"On the first issue of prescription, I submit that..."
"Turning to the second issue of liability..."

**Answering Questions from the Bench**

Answer the question directly before elaborating. If you do not know the answer, say so candidly: "I am not able to assist the court on that point from the authorities before me." Never give a speculative answer as a definitive one.

**Citing Case Law in Argument**

"My Lord, I rely on the decision of the Supreme Court in *Deweras Farm (Pvt) Ltd v Standard Chartered Bank Zimbabwe Ltd* (SC 78/2018), which held that the two-pronged test for setting aside a default judgment requires both an acceptable explanation for the default and a bona fide defence. The Respondent has satisfied neither."

**Conceding Points**

Credibility with the court is built by conceding points that cannot be won. Attempting to defend indefensible positions damages counsel's credibility on stronger points.

### Part 4: Review Proceedings — Administrative Law

**Nature of Review**

Review differs from appeal. On review, the court does not reconsider the merits de novo but examines whether the decision-maker acted lawfully, reasonably and procedurally fairly (section 68 of the Constitution; Administrative Justice Act [Chapter 10:28]).

**Grounds of Review**

Under the Administrative Justice Act and the common law, grounds of review include—
(a) ultra vires — the decision-maker lacked jurisdiction;
(b) breach of natural justice — audi alteram partem or nemo judex in sua causa;
(c) error of law — the decision-maker misinterpreted the applicable law;
(d) unreasonableness — the decision was so unreasonable that no reasonable decision-maker could have made it;
(e) bad faith or improper purpose.

**Procedural Requirements for Review**

A review application must be brought promptly. Delay may defeat the application on grounds of laches or, under statute, if the application is out of time.

The record of the administrative proceedings must be obtained and filed.

### Part 5: Constitutional Litigation

**Bringing a Constitutional Challenge**

A party wishing to challenge the constitutionality of a law or conduct must—
(a) give notice in the pleadings that a constitutional point is being raised;
(b) give notice to the Attorney General (who may intervene as amicus curiae);
(c) refer the matter to the Constitutional Court if the challenge relates to an Act of Parliament.

**Standing to Bring Constitutional Claims**

Section 85 of the Constitution confers broad standing. Any person, acting in their own interests or in the public interest or as a member of a group, may approach the court for relief.

**Constitutional Remedies**

The Constitutional Court may—
(a) declare a law invalid;
(b) order just compensation for a constitutional violation;
(c) issue any order that is just and equitable.
