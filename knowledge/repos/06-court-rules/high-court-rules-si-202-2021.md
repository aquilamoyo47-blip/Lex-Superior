# High Court Rules, SI 202 of 2021
## Zimbabwe — High Court Rules 2021 (as amended)
## Statutory Instrument 202 of 2021

### Part 1: Preliminary

**Rule 1: Short title**
These rules may be cited as the High Court Rules, 2021.

**Rule 2: Interpretation**
In these rules—
"action" means a legal proceeding in the High Court by which a cause or matter is brought before the court for trial;
"application" means a legal proceeding in the High Court by which a cause or matter is brought before the court on motion;
"defendant" means the person against whom an action is brought;
"judgment" means any final decision of the court in an action;
"order" means any final or interlocutory decision of the court in an application or in the course of an action;
"plaintiff" means the person who brings an action;
"pleadings" means the declaration and plea in an action;
"process" means any writ, summons, notice, or other document issued from the court in the course of proceedings;
"registry" means the office of the Registrar of the High Court.

### Part 2: Commencement of Action

**Rule 8: Summons**
(1) Every action in the High Court shall be commenced by service on the defendant of a summons signed by the Registrar or by an attorney on behalf of the plaintiff.
(2) A summons shall—
(a) be in Form 2;
(b) contain a concise statement of the cause of action;
(c) state the relief claimed;
(d) require the defendant to enter an appearance to defend within the period specified in the summons.

**Rule 9: Simple summons and combined summons**
(1) A simple summons shall be used where the claim is—
(a) for a debt or liquidated demand in money;
(b) for the delivery of goods for which a liquidated sum can be paid as an alternative.
(2) A combined summons shall be used in all other cases and shall be accompanied by a declaration.

**Rule 10: Service of summons**
(1) Service of a summons shall be effected—
(a) personally on the defendant by delivering a copy thereof to the defendant;
(b) at the defendant's place of residence or business by delivering a copy to a person apparently residing or employed there;
(c) by post, where the defendant has agreed to accept service by post.
(2) Where service cannot be effected in the manner prescribed, the court may on application order substituted service.

**Rule 11: Appearance to defend**
(1) A defendant who has been served with a summons and who intends to oppose the plaintiff's claim shall within—
(a) 10 days after service, if served within Zimbabwe;
(b) 21 days after service, if served outside Zimbabwe but within Africa;
(c) 30 days after service, if served elsewhere;
enter an appearance to defend.
(2) An appearance to defend shall be in Form 3 and shall be filed with the Registrar and a copy served on the plaintiff.

### Part 3: Pleadings

**Rule 18: Declaration**
(1) Where a combined summons has been served on the defendant, the plaintiff shall deliver to the defendant a declaration setting out the material facts on which the cause of action relies.
(2) A declaration shall be delivered within 15 days after the defendant has entered an appearance to defend.

**Rule 19: Plea**
(1) A defendant shall deliver to the plaintiff a plea setting out the nature of the defence within 15 days after delivery of the declaration.
(2) A plea shall clearly and concisely state which allegations in the declaration are admitted and which are denied.
(3) Where the defendant raises a special plea, such as prescription, lis pendens, res judicata, or lack of jurisdiction, the special plea shall be raised at the commencement of the plea.

**Rule 20: Counterclaim**
(1) A defendant may, with the plea, deliver a counterclaim against the plaintiff.
(2) A counterclaim shall be treated as if it were an action brought by the defendant against the plaintiff.

**Rule 21: Replication**
(1) The plaintiff may, within 10 days after service of the plea, deliver a replication addressing the new matter raised in the plea.
(2) No further pleadings shall be delivered without leave of court.

**Rule 22: Exception**
(1) A party may except to a pleading on the grounds that—
(a) it lacks averments necessary to sustain an action or defence, as the case may be; or
(b) it is vague and embarrassing.
(2) Before filing an exception, the excepting party shall notify the other party of the intended exception and allow the other party 10 days to amend.

### Part 4: Applications (Motions)

**Rule 241: Application procedure — General**
(1) Applications to the court shall be made on notice of motion supported by an affidavit.
(2) The notice of motion shall state—
(a) the order or relief sought;
(b) the date and time at which the application will be heard;
(c) the grounds upon which the application is based.

**Rule 242: Urgent applications**
(1) A party seeking urgent relief shall file a certificate of urgency setting out the grounds of urgency.
(2) An urgent application may be set down on not less than 24 hours' notice to the respondent, or in exceptional circumstances, on no notice.
(3) The court shall determine whether the matter warrants urgent treatment and may, if satisfied, dispense with the notice period.

**Rule 243: Opposing affidavit**
(1) A respondent who wishes to oppose an application shall file an opposing affidavit within 10 days of service of the notice of motion.
(2) The opposing affidavit shall deal specifically with each allegation in the founding affidavit.

**Rule 244: Replying affidavit**
The applicant may file a replying affidavit within 5 days of service of the opposing affidavit, dealing only with new matters raised in the opposing affidavit.

### Part 5: Summary Judgment

**Rule 64: Application for summary judgment**
(1) Where a defendant has entered an appearance to defend, the plaintiff may apply to the court for summary judgment on the ground that there is no bona fide defence.
(2) An application for summary judgment shall be supported by an affidavit verifying the cause of action and the amount claimed, and stating that in the deponent's belief there is no bona fide defence.

**Rule 65: Opposition to summary judgment**
(1) The defendant may oppose summary judgment by filing an affidavit setting out the facts which constitute the defence.
(2) The affidavit must disclose a bona fide defence that is not purely technical.
(3) The defendant may also deliver the opposing affidavit personally or through counsel at the hearing of the application.

**Rule 66: Judgment on summary judgment application**
(1) On a summary judgment application, the court may—
(a) grant summary judgment for the plaintiff;
(b) dismiss the application;
(c) grant leave to defend unconditionally;
(d) grant leave to defend on condition of payment of security.

### Part 6: Default Judgment

**Rule 67: Default judgment**
(1) Where a defendant fails to enter an appearance to defend, the plaintiff may apply for default judgment.
(2) An application for default judgment shall be made to the Registrar where the claim is for a liquidated amount, or to the court in all other cases.

**Rule 68: Requirements for default judgment**
(1) Before default judgment is entered, proof of service must be filed.
(2) Where the claim is unliquidated, the plaintiff must give proof of the damages claimed.

**Rule 69: Setting aside default judgment**
(1) A defendant against whom default judgment has been given may apply to the court to have the judgment set aside.
(2) The application shall be made on notice supported by an affidavit setting out—
(a) a reasonable and acceptable explanation for the default; and
(b) a bona fide defence to the plaintiff's claim.

### Part 7: Trial

**Rule 110: Notice of trial**
(1) Where a matter has been set down for trial, the plaintiff shall give not less than 10 days' notice to the defendant.
(2) A matter shall be set down by filing a notice of trial with the Registrar, who shall allocate a date.

**Rule 111: Pre-trial conference**
(1) In all defended matters, the parties shall attend a pre-trial conference before the matter is set down for trial.
(2) At the pre-trial conference, the parties shall—
(a) attempt to settle the matter;
(b) agree on the issues of fact and law for trial;
(c) agree on a bundle of documents.

**Rule 112: Trial procedure**
(1) The plaintiff shall open the case by presenting the evidence in support of the claim.
(2) Each party may call witnesses who shall be examined, cross-examined, and re-examined.
(3) After all evidence has been led, counsel shall address the court on the facts and the law.

### Part 8: Appeals

**Rule 121: Appeals from magistrates court**
(1) An appeal from a judgment of a magistrates court to the High Court shall be lodged within 15 days of the judgment appealed against.
(2) The appellant shall file a notice of appeal setting out the grounds of appeal.

**Rule 122: Record of proceedings**
(1) The clerk of the magistrates court shall prepare a record of the proceedings and transmit it to the High Court.
(2) The appellant shall pay the prescribed fee for the preparation of the record.

### Part 9: Costs

**Rule 123: Costs**
(1) The costs of an action or application shall follow the event unless the court otherwise orders.
(2) The court may make a special costs order where—
(a) a party has been vexatious or unreasonable;
(b) a party has wasted costs by unnecessary application.

**Rule 124: Scale of costs**
(1) Costs shall be taxed by the taxing master on the scale prescribed by the Rules Committee.
(2) The court may order costs on an attorney-client scale in exceptional cases.

### Order 32: Ejectment

**Rule 227: Application for ejectment**
(1) A person who is entitled to occupation of land or premises and who claims that another person is unlawfully in occupation may apply to the court for an order for ejectment.
(2) The application shall be on notice to the person in occupation.
(3) The court shall not grant an order for ejectment unless the applicant has established a clear right to occupation.

### Order 33: Interdict

**Rule 232: Application for interdict**
(1) A person who wishes to prevent another person from doing something or to compel another person to do something may apply to the court for an interdict.
(2) The applicant must establish—
(a) a clear right;
(b) an injury actually committed or reasonably apprehended; and
(c) the absence of an adequate alternative remedy.
(3) In urgent cases, an interdict may be granted on an ex parte basis, but must be served on the respondent as soon as possible.
