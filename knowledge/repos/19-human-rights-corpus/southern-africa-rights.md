# Southern Africa Human Rights and Civil Procedure
## Evidentiary and Constitutional Procedure — Zimbabwe Focus

### Evidence in Civil Proceedings

#### The Civil Evidence Act [Chapter 8:01]

**Section 5: Admissibility of documents**
(1) In any civil proceedings, a statement contained in a document shall, subject to this section, be admissible as evidence of any fact stated therein of which direct oral evidence would be admissible, if the document is or forms part of a record compiled in the ordinary course of any trade, business, profession or other occupation.

(2) The conditions for admissibility are—
(a) the maker of the statement had, or may reasonably be supposed to have had, personal knowledge of the matters dealt with in the statement; and
(b) any person who had personal knowledge of the matters dealt with in the document was acting under a duty to supply the information.

**Section 6: Evidence of computer records**
(1) In any civil proceedings, a statement reproduced by a computer shall be admissible as evidence of any fact stated therein if the computer was used regularly to store information of the kind contained in the statement.

**Section 7: Affidavit evidence**
Evidence by affidavit is admissible in motion proceedings. The deponent must be a competent witness. The deponent swears to the truth of the facts stated.

#### Admissibility of Expert Evidence

Expert witnesses may give opinion evidence in Zimbabwe civil proceedings where—
(a) the matter is one on which expert evidence is admissible (technical, scientific, medical);
(b) the expert is properly qualified;
(c) the expert's opinion is relevant to a material fact in issue.

Experts must be independent; they owe a duty to the court, not to the party retaining them.

#### Privilege in Civil Proceedings

**Attorney-client privilege (legal professional privilege)**
Communications between an attorney and client, made in the context of seeking or giving legal advice, are privileged and may not be disclosed without the client's consent.

**Litigation privilege**
Documents brought into existence for the dominant purpose of anticipated litigation are privileged.

**Without prejudice rule**
Communications made in the course of genuine attempts to settle a dispute ("without prejudice" communications) are privileged and may not be used as evidence.

### International Human Rights Standards — Civil Dimension

#### International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR)

Zimbabwe ratified the ICCPR. Relevant provisions affecting civil proceedings:

**Article 14 — Right to a fair trial**
All persons shall be equal before the courts and tribunals. In the determination of any civil claim or any criminal charge, everyone shall be entitled to a fair and public hearing before a competent, independent and impartial tribunal established by law.

**Article 17 — Right to privacy**
(1) No one shall be subjected to arbitrary or unlawful interference with his privacy, family, home or correspondence.
(2) Everyone has the right to the protection of the law against such interference or attacks.

Zimbabwe's common law of delict (actio iniuriarum) gives effect to this right by providing remedies for invasion of privacy.

#### African Charter on Human and Peoples' Rights

Zimbabwe is a state party to the African Charter. Relevant civil rights provisions:

**Article 7 — Right to a fair trial**
Every individual shall have the right to have his cause heard. This comprises:
(a) the right to an appeal to competent national organs against acts of violating fundamental rights as recognised and guaranteed by conventions, laws, regulations and customs in force;
(b) the right to be presumed innocent until proved guilty by a competent court or tribunal;
(c) the right to defence, including the right to be defended by counsel of his choice;
(d) the right to be tried within a reasonable time by an impartial court or tribunal.

**Article 14 — Right to property**
The right to property shall be guaranteed. It may only be encroached upon in the interest of public need or in the general interest of the community and in accordance with the provisions of appropriate laws.

### Zimbabwe — Procedural Fairness in Administrative Law

**The audi alteram partem principle**

The right to be heard (audi alteram partem) is a cardinal principle of procedural fairness in Zimbabwe law. It requires that—
(a) a person be informed of the case against them;
(b) a person be given an adequate opportunity to present their side;
(c) the decision-maker be impartial (nemo judex in sua causa).

The Constitutional Court has affirmed that these principles are now constitutionally entrenched by section 68 of the Constitution.

**Test for procedural unfairness leading to review**

In *Nyandoro v Public Service Commission* HH 56/2021, the court restated the test:
A decision-maker is in breach of the duty to act fairly if the procedure adopted was such that a person in the applicant's position, knowing of the decision-maker's duty to act fairly, would reasonably have considered the procedure to be unfair.

### Human Rights Defenders — Procedural Protections

Human rights defenders who are parties to litigation in Zimbabwe's Superior Courts are entitled to all procedural protections afforded by the Constitution, including—
(a) the right to a fair and public hearing (section 69);
(b) access to justice (section 69(3));
(c) the right to legal representation (section 69(2));
(d) protection from unreasonable delays in the determination of civil matters.
