### Prescription Act [Chapter 8:11]

**Part I: Prescription of Debts**

**11. Prescription of debts**
(1) Subject to the provisions of this Part, a debt shall be extinguished by prescription after the lapse of the period which in terms of the relevant law applies in respect of the prescription of such debt.
(2) The periods of prescription of debts are the following—
(a) thirty years in respect of—
(i) any debt secured by a mortgage bond;
(ii) any judgment debt;
(iii) any debt in respect of any taxation imposed or levied by or under any law;
(iv) any debt owed to the State;
(b) six years in respect of any debt arising from a bill of exchange or other negotiable instrument or from a notarial contract;
(c) three years in respect of any other debt.

**12. Commencement of prescription**
(1) Subject to the provisions of sections 13 to 15, prescription shall commence to run as soon as the debt is due.
(2) A debt shall not be deemed to be due until the creditor has knowledge of the identity of the debtor and of the facts from which the debt arises.

**13. Completion of prescription**
(1) Prescription shall not be completed if—
(a) the debtor acknowledges liability to the creditor;
(b) the creditor serves process on the debtor for the purpose of interrupting prescription.
(2) An acknowledgement of liability shall interrupt prescription, which shall then commence to run afresh.

**14. Interruption of prescription**
(1) The running of prescription shall be interrupted by the service on the debtor of any process whereby the creditor claims payment of the debt.
(2) Service of process shall interrupt prescription even if such process is void on account of some irregularity.

### Companies and Other Business Entities Act [Chapter 24:31] — COBE Act

**Part I: Interpretation**

**2. Interpretation**
In this Act—
"company" means a private business corporation or public business corporation incorporated under this Act;
"director" means a person appointed to perform the functions of a director of a company;
"legal personality" means the separate legal personality conferred on a company upon its incorporation;
"members" means the shareholders of a company;
"public business corporation" means a company registered as a public business corporation under this Act;
"private business corporation" means a company registered as a private business corporation under this Act.

**Part II: Formation and Registration**

**8. Formation of private business corporation**
(1) One or more persons may form a private business corporation by—
(a) preparing a memorandum of incorporation;
(b) filing the memorandum with the Registrar of Companies; and
(c) paying the prescribed fee.
(2) A private business corporation shall have—
(a) not more than fifty members; and
(b) restrictions on the transferability of its shares.

**9. Memorandum of incorporation**
(1) The memorandum of incorporation of a company shall set out—
(a) the name of the company;
(b) the purpose of the company;
(c) the authorized share capital;
(d) the rights and obligations of shareholders.

**Part V: Directors and Officers**

**65. Duties of directors**
(1) A director of a company shall—
(a) act in good faith and in the best interests of the company;
(b) exercise the powers of the director for a proper purpose;
(c) act with the degree of care, skill and diligence that may reasonably be expected of a person carrying out the same functions in relation to the company as those carried out by that director.

**66. Liability of directors**
(1) A director who contravenes this Act or the memorandum of incorporation of the company shall be liable to the company for any loss, damage or costs sustained by the company as a result of any breach by the director of a duty contemplated in section 65.
(2) A director who has acted fraudulently or dishonestly shall be personally liable.

### Insolvency Act [Chapter 6:04]

**2. Insolvency defined**
For the purposes of this Act, a person is insolvent if that person is unable to pay his or her debts as they become due in the ordinary course of business, or if the value of that person's assets is less than the amount of that person's liabilities.

**8. Application for sequestration**
(1) Any creditor who has a liquidated claim of not less than the prescribed minimum amount against a debtor may apply to the High Court for the provisional sequestration of the debtor's estate.
(2) The application shall be supported by an affidavit setting out—
(a) the facts giving rise to the creditor's claim;
(b) the grounds upon which the applicant believes the debtor to be insolvent.

**9. Provisional order of sequestration**
(1) On an application for provisional sequestration, the court may grant a provisional order if it is satisfied that—
(a) the applicant has established a prima facie case for sequestration; and
(b) there is reason to believe that it will be to the advantage of creditors if the debtor's estate is sequestrated.
(2) A provisional order shall call upon the debtor to appear and show cause why a final order of sequestration should not be granted.

**12. Effect of sequestration**
Upon the granting of a final order of sequestration—
(a) the insolvent's estate vests in the Master of the High Court;
(b) the insolvent loses the right to administer his or her estate;
(c) all pending actions and proceedings against the insolvent shall be stayed.
