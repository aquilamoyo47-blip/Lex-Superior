# Zimbabwe Constitutional Jurisprudence
## Constitutional Rights — Case Summaries and Analysis

### Constitutional Court — Key Civil Decisions

#### Due Process and Fair Hearing

**Mudzuru & Another v Minister of Justice, Legal and Parliamentary Affairs & Others CCZ 12/2015**
The Constitutional Court held that forced child marriage violated constitutional rights under sections 56 (equality), 61 (freedom of expression via education), and 78 (marriage). This landmark decision illustrates the Constitutional Court's willingness to protect fundamental rights proactively.

**Mupamhanga v Minister of Home Affairs & Another CCZ 14/2022**
Application for declaration of constitutional invalidity of detention provisions in the Immigration Act. The court considered sections 49 and 50 of the Constitution relating to liberty. The court held that detention must be authorised by law and subject to judicial oversight.

#### Property Rights

**Mike Campbell (Pvt) Ltd and Others v Republic of Zimbabwe SADC (T) Case No. 2/2007**
*[Historical — SADC Tribunal, now suspended]*
The SADC Tribunal held that Zimbabwe's land reform programme violated the SADC Treaty because it was discriminatory and denied access to justice. This decision precipitated Zimbabwe's withdrawal from the SADC Tribunal protocol.

**Benefit to note for practitioners:** Section 71 of the Constitution of Zimbabwe 2013 now provides constitutional protection for property rights, albeit subject to the public interest limitations in section 71(2).

#### Administrative Justice

**Fawcett Security Operations v Omar Meya & Others HH 345/2020**
The High Court held that an employee dismissed without being afforded an opportunity to be heard had been denied the constitutional right to administrative justice under section 68 of the Constitution. The employer was ordered to reinstate the employee and pay back pay.

**R v Disciplinary Committee of the Zimbabwe Stock Exchange, ex parte Intercash Corporation Ltd HH 123/2019**
The court reviewed a disciplinary decision of the Zimbabwe Stock Exchange. The court held that the duty of procedural fairness (audi alteram partem) applied to professional disciplinary proceedings. The disciplinary committee was required to give Intercash adequate notice of the case against it and an opportunity to respond before imposing sanctions.

### High Court — Constitutional Litigation

#### Right to Legal Representation

**Criminal Lawyers Association of Zimbabwe v Minister of Justice HH 234/2020**
The court held that the right to legal representation under section 69(2) of the Constitution is not absolute, but applies in all matters where substantial injustice would otherwise result.

#### Section 68 — Administrative Justice

**Principles established by the courts:**

1. Legality: An administrative act must be authorised by law.
2. Rationality: The decision must be rationally connected to the facts and the purpose of the empowering provision.
3. Reasonableness: The decision must not be so unreasonable that no reasonable decision-maker could have made it.
4. Procedural fairness: The affected person must be given notice and an opportunity to be heard.
5. Proportionality: The remedy must be proportionate to the infringement.

#### Constitutional Damages

Under section 85(1) of the Constitution, the court may award constitutional damages for violation of rights. The quantum of constitutional damages is at the court's discretion, having regard to the nature and extent of the violation.

### Constitutional Court Jurisdiction

**Section 167: Jurisdiction of Constitutional Court**
The Constitutional Court—
(a) is the highest court in all constitutional matters;
(b) decides only constitutional matters and issues connected with constitutional decisions;
(c) makes the final decision whether a matter is a constitutional matter.

**Referral from other courts:**
If a constitutional issue arises in proceedings before another court, the court may refer the matter to the Constitutional Court.

**Certification of constitutional matters:**
The Constitutional Court may decline to hear a constitutional matter if it is not in the interests of justice or if the matter is not of sufficient importance.

### Locus Standi in Constitutional Proceedings

**Section 85: Enforcement of fundamental human rights and freedoms**
Any of the following persons, namely—
(a) any person acting in their own interests;
(b) any person acting on behalf of another person who cannot act in their own name;
(c) any person acting as a member, or in the interests, of a group or class of persons;
(d) any person acting in the public interest;
(e) any association acting in the interests of its members;
is entitled to approach a court alleging that a fundamental right or freedom in the Declaration of Rights has been, is being or is likely to be infringed, and the court may grant appropriate relief, including a declaration of rights and an award of compensation.

**Note:** This broad standing requirement contrasts with the more restrictive locus standi requirements in ordinary civil proceedings, where a litigant must have a direct and substantial interest in the subject matter.
