# Voet's Commentary on the Pandects
## Joannes Voet — Commentarius ad Pandectas
## Public Domain — Foundational Roman-Dutch Law Text
## Translated and adapted for Zimbabwean legal practice

### On the Law of Obligations

**General Principles of Obligation (Obligationes in General)**

An obligation, in the sense of the Roman jurists, is a bond of law by which we are necessitated to perform something according to the laws of our state. Obligations arise from contract, quasi-contract, delict, or quasi-delict.

**Natural and Civil Obligations**

A natural obligation is one which the law of nature recognises, even though positive law affords no civil remedy. A civil obligation, on the other hand, is one which the civil law both recognises and for which it provides a remedy.

In Roman-Dutch law, which forms the common law of Zimbabwe, obligations are classified as—
(i) contractual obligations (arising from agreement);
(ii) quasi-contractual obligations (arising from circumstances resembling contract);
(iii) delictual obligations (arising from wrongful acts); and
(iv) quasi-delictual obligations.

### On Contracts (De Contractibus)

**Consensus as foundation of contract**

A valid contract requires consensus ad idem — a meeting of minds between the parties. There must be—
(a) an offer by one party;
(b) acceptance of that offer by the other party; and
(c) the animus contrahendi — the intention to conclude a legally binding agreement.

**Essentialia, naturalia and accidentalia**

Every contract has essentialia (essential elements without which it cannot exist), naturalia (terms implied by law) and accidentalia (terms expressly agreed upon by the parties).

**Voet on the exceptio non adimpleti contractus**

The exceptio non adimpleti contractus — the defence of the unfulfilled contract — is that exception which arises when one party to a reciprocal contract sues for performance before performing or tendering performance of his own obligation. This principle is fundamental to bilateral contracts in Zimbabwe's common law.

### On Suretyship (De Fideiussione)

**Nature of suretyship**

Suretyship is a contract whereby one person (the surety) binds himself to a creditor to perform the obligation of a principal debtor, should the debtor fail to do so.

**Benefits available to a surety**

In Roman-Dutch law, a surety may avail himself of:
(i) the beneficium excussionis — the right to require the creditor first to proceed against the principal debtor;
(ii) the beneficium divisionis — the right to require the creditor to divide the debt among co-sureties;
(iii) the beneficium cedendarum actionum — the right to require the creditor to cede his claims against the debtor to the surety upon payment.

These benefits may be waived by agreement, and in Zimbabwe commercial practice, it is standard for suretyship agreements to contain renunciation of all three benefits.

### On Prescription (De Praescriptione)

**Voet's treatment of prescription**

Prescription is the acquisition or extinction of rights through the lapse of time. Prescription serves important public policy purposes: it brings certainty, prevents stale claims, and protects defendants from the injustice of defending against claims long after events have occurred and evidence is lost.

**Interruption of prescription**

Prescription is interrupted by—
(a) judicial demand — the service of summons or other process;
(b) acknowledgement of liability by the debtor.

Upon interruption, prescription begins to run afresh.

**Suspension of prescription**

Prescription does not run against a person under legal disability (minor or person of unsound mind).

### On Property (De Rebus)

**Distinction between real and personal rights**

A real right (jus in rem) is enforceable against the world at large and attaches to the property itself. A personal right (jus in personam) is enforceable only against a specific person.

In Zimbabwe, real rights in immovable property are registered in the Deeds Office in terms of the Deeds Registries Act [Chapter 20:05]. Registration is constitutive of real rights in immovable property.

**Ownership and possession**

Ownership is the most complete real right. Possession is the physical control of a thing with the intent to hold it as owner (animus domini). The interdict possessorium (possessory interdict) protects possession as against wrongful interference.

### On Delict (De Delictis)

**Aquilian liability**

The Lex Aquilia provides the foundation for delictual liability in Zimbabwe's common law. The requirements for Aquilian liability are:
(i) an act or omission;
(ii) wrongfulness;
(iii) fault (dolus or culpa);
(iv) causation (factual and legal); and
(v) harm.

**Pure economic loss**

Delictual liability for pure economic loss is recognised in Zimbabwe's common law but is more restricted than liability for physical injury or property damage. The wrongfulness element requires that the harm fall within the class of interests which the law protects against the defendant's conduct.
