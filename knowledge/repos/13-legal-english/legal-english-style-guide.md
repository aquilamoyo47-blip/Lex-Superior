# Legal English — Style Guide for Zimbabwe Superior Courts
## Plain Language and Formal Legal Drafting

### Part 1: Principles of Legal Drafting

**1.1 Clarity and Precision**

Legal documents must be clear, precise and unambiguous. The primary duty of a legal drafter is to express the client's or court's intention in language that leaves no room for doubt.

Principles of clarity:
(a) Use short sentences where possible.
(b) Use the active voice unless the passive voice is necessary.
(c) Avoid double negatives.
(d) Define technical terms.
(e) Use consistent terminology throughout the document.

**1.2 Formal Register**

Pleadings and heads of argument in the Superior Courts of Zimbabwe must be drafted in the formal register. This means—
(a) No contractions (use "shall not" not "shan't").
(b) No colloquialisms.
(c) Formal pronoun usage ("the Applicant" rather than "my client").
(d) Passive constructions where appropriate ("It is submitted that..." rather than "I argue that...").

**1.3 Precision in Legal Language**

Words have specific meanings in law. Common drafting errors include:
- Confusing "and" with "or" in statutes and agreements — these have fundamentally different effects.
- Using "may" when "shall" is intended — "may" is permissive, "shall" is mandatory.
- Using "including" without "without limitation" — "including" may be construed as exhaustive.

### Part 2: Structure of Legal Documents

**2.1 Pleadings**

Well-drafted pleadings:
(a) Are divided into numbered paragraphs;
(b) State each material fact in a separate paragraph;
(c) Distinguish between facts and legal conclusions;
(d) Avoid narrative and surplusage;
(e) Identify the cause of action clearly.

**2.2 Affidavits**

An affidavit must:
(a) Be sworn or affirmed before a commissioner of oaths;
(b) Contain only facts within the deponent's personal knowledge (except where inference or information and belief is indicated);
(c) Clearly distinguish between personal knowledge and hearsay;
(d) Be in the first person.

**2.3 Heads of Argument**

Heads of argument should:
(a) Begin with a concise introduction setting out the nature of the dispute;
(b) State the legal issues clearly and separately;
(c) Set out submissions under each issue with supporting authorities;
(d) Cross-reference the record;
(e) Conclude with a clear statement of the order sought.

### Part 3: Common Legal Terms

**Locus standi (standing)**
A party must have locus standi to bring proceedings. This requires a direct and substantial interest in the subject matter of the proceedings.

**Cause of action**
A cause of action is the complete set of facts which gives a party the right to claim relief. All elements of the cause of action must be pleaded.

**Prima facie case**
A prima facie case is a case which, if not answered, would entitle the party asserting it to succeed. The threshold is lower than on a balance of probabilities.

**Balance of probabilities**
The standard of proof in civil proceedings. A party who bears the onus must establish their case on a balance of probabilities — meaning it is more probable than not that their version is correct.

**Onus of proof**
In civil proceedings, the onus is on the party who asserts a positive fact. The maxim is: *ei incumbit probatio qui dicit, non qui negat* (the burden of proof lies on the one who asserts, not on the one who denies).

### Part 4: Drafting Court Documents

**4.1 The Declaration**

A declaration must plead—
(a) The parties and their capacities;
(b) The material facts giving rise to the cause of action, in chronological order;
(c) The legal basis of the claim;
(d) The relief sought.

**4.2 The Plea**

A plea must—
(a) Specifically admit or deny each allegation in the declaration;
(b) State the reasons for each denial;
(c) Set out the defendant's version of any denied facts;
(d) Raise any special pleas at the outset.

**4.3 Heads of Argument — Style**

Counsel appearing in the Superior Courts of Zimbabwe should:
(a) Address the court as "The Honourable Court" or "this Court";
(b) Refer to the presiding officer as "Your Lordship" (High Court) or "Your Lordships" (Supreme Court / Constitutional Court);
(c) Refer to opposing counsel as "my learned colleague";
(d) Refer to one's own client as "the Applicant/Plaintiff/Appellant" not "my client";
(e) Use "submit" rather than "argue" — counsel submits to the court; counsel does not argue.

### Part 5: Legal Latin in Zimbabwe Superior Courts

**Appropriate use of Latin maxims**

Latin maxims remain in use in Zimbabwe's Superior Courts as a precise shorthand for legal principles. However, every Latin maxim should be accompanied by its English equivalent or explanation, particularly for litigants in person.

Common maxims:
- *In re* — in the matter of
- *Ex parte* — on behalf of one party only (without notice to the other side)
- *Locus standi in judicio* — standing to appear in court
- *Audi alteram partem* — hear the other side (a fundamental principle of natural justice)
- *Nemo judex in sua causa* — no one may be a judge in their own cause
- *Ex abundanti cautela* — from an abundance of caution
- *Sine die* — without a day (indefinitely adjourned)
- *Pro se* — for oneself (self-represented)
- *In limine* — at the threshold (preliminary objection)
