# Southern African Civil Procedure — Key Judgments
## Zimbabwe, South Africa, and SADC Jurisprudence

### Zimbabwe Civil Procedure — Leading Cases

#### Summary Judgment

**Herbstein & Van Winsen, Civil Practice of the High Courts of South Africa (5th ed)**
*[Persuasive authority in Zimbabwe]*

The test for summary judgment was established in **Maharaj v Barclays National Bank Ltd 1976 (1) SA 418 (A)**:
The defendant must, in order to succeed in obtaining leave to defend:
1. Fully disclose the nature and grounds of the defence;
2. Show that the defence is not fictitious or merely raised to delay;
3. The defence must be bona fide.

#### Special Pleas

**Prescription — test for running of prescription:**
In *Zimbabwe Revenue Authority v Murasi* (unreported, HH 234/2019):
The court held that prescription commences running when the creditor has knowledge of the identity of the debtor and the facts from which the debt arises, per section 12(2) of the Prescription Act [Chapter 8:11].

**Res judicata:**
The elements of res judicata in Zimbabwe were restated in *Madhatter Mining Co (Pvt) Ltd v Kuvimba Mining House (Pvt) Ltd* (SC 45/2020):
(a) same parties;
(b) same cause of action; and
(c) same relief claimed.

**Lis pendens:**
In *Chawafambira v Chawafambira* (HH 112/2019):
The court restated the requirements for a special plea of lis pendens:
(a) the same parties must be engaged in the earlier proceedings;
(b) the subject matter must be the same;
(c) the earlier proceedings must be pending before a court of competent jurisdiction.

#### Default Judgment — Setting Aside

**Test for setting aside default judgment:**
In *Deweras Farm (Pvt) Ltd v Standard Chartered Bank Zimbabwe Ltd* (SC 78/2018), the Supreme Court confirmed the two-pronged test:
1. A reasonable and acceptable explanation for the default; and
2. A bona fide defence on the merits.

The court emphasised that both requirements must be satisfied; satisfying one alone is insufficient.

#### Interdicts

**Requirements for a final interdict** (*Setlogelo v Setlogelo 1914 AD 221, followed in Zimbabwe*):
1. A clear right (not prima facie established);
2. Injury actually committed or reasonably apprehended; and
3. The absence of an adequate alternative remedy.

**Requirements for a temporary interdict:**
1. A right that prima facie appears to exist;
2. A well-grounded apprehension of irreparable harm;
3. The balance of convenience favours granting of the order; and
4. No adequate alternative remedy.

*Airfield Investments (Pvt) Ltd v Minister of Lands, Agriculture & Rural Resettlement* 2004 (2) ZLR 433 (H): The court emphasised that the applicant must show a prima facie right — not necessarily a strong one — but must demonstrate that the apprehension of harm is well-grounded.

#### Declaratory Orders

**Jurisdiction to grant declaratory order:**
Under section 14 of the High Court Act [Chapter 7:06], the High Court may in its discretion grant declaratory orders, even if no consequential relief is sought. The applicant must, however, be an interested person — one having a direct and substantial interest in the subject matter of the litigation.

*Zimbabwe Open University v Minister of Education* (HH 456/2021): An applicant for a declaratory order must show an actual controversy, not a hypothetical dispute.

#### Contempt of Court

**Willfulness as element of contempt:**
In *Chaparadza v Chaparadza* (HH 89/2020), the court held that to establish contempt, the applicant must prove beyond reasonable doubt:
(a) the existence of an order;
(b) knowledge of the order by the alleged contemnor;
(c) that the contemnor failed to comply; and
(d) that the non-compliance was wilful and mala fide.

#### Writ of Execution — Immovable Property

**Standard Bank Zimbabwe Ltd v Dzimba** (HH 201/2019):
Before granting an order declaring immovable property specially executable, the court must conduct a proportionality enquiry. The court must consider whether the extent of the encumbrance justifies execution, particularly where the property constitutes the judgment debtor's primary residence.

### South African Authorities (Persuasive in Zimbabwe)

**Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd 1984 (3) SA 623 (A)**:
Where there is a dispute of fact in motion proceedings, the matter must be decided on the version of the respondent, unless the respondent's version is so clearly untenable or dishonest that it cannot be believed, in which case the court may decide in favour of the applicant.

**National Credit Regulator v Opperman 2013 (2) SA 1 (CC)**:
*[Comparative — not applicable in Zimbabwe]*: However, the principle that statutory provisions must be read in their proper context, giving effect to the legislative purpose, is applicable as a principle of statutory interpretation in Zimbabwe.
