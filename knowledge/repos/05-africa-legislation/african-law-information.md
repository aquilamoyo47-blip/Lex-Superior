## Curated African Legal Texts — Civil Law Focus

### Civil Procedure Acts Across SADC

#### South Africa — Superior Courts Act No. 10 of 2013 (Persuasive Authority)

The South African Superior Courts Act consolidates the law relating to the constitution, powers and functioning of the High Court, Supreme Court of Appeal, and Constitutional Court.

Key provisions of relevance as persuasive authority in Zimbabwe:

**Part I: General Provisions**
Section 4: Jurisdiction of the High Court
- The High Court has jurisdiction over all persons and matters within its area of jurisdiction.
- Subject matter jurisdiction extends to all civil and criminal matters.

**Section 8: Power to develop the common law**
Every court must develop the common law, as envisaged in the Constitution, in a manner that promotes the spirit, purport and objects of the Bill of Rights.

#### Botswana — High Court Act Cap. 04:02

The High Court of Botswana exercises a similar jurisdiction to that of Zimbabwe's High Court. Botswana shares the Roman-Dutch common law foundation and cases from Botswana are sometimes cited as persuasive authority in Zimbabwe.

The requirements for a mandament van spolie in Botswana were restated in *Kanyana v Kanyana* [2018] BWHC 4:
(i) the applicant was in peaceful and undisturbed possession;
(ii) the respondent unlawfully deprived the applicant of that possession;
These two elements suffice — the court does not inquire into who has a better right to possess.

#### Zambia — High Court Act Cap. 27

Zambia's High Court Act, which shares a common English colonial heritage with Zimbabwe's, provides useful comparisons on procedural matters. Zambian cases are occasionally cited in Zimbabwe.

#### Namibia — High Court Act No. 16 of 1990

Namibia retained South Africa's Roman-Dutch common law at independence. Namibian High Court decisions applying Roman-Dutch principles carry significant persuasive weight in Zimbabwe, particularly in matters of commercial law and civil procedure.

#### Default Judgment Thresholds

In all SADC jurisdictions, default judgment requires proof of—
(a) valid service of process;
(b) failure to defend within the prescribed time;
(c) where unliquidated: proof of quantum.

### Open Legal Standards for Africa

**African Legislation Markup Language (AfricanLML)**

Laws.Africa has developed the African Legislation Markup Language, based on the international standard Akoma Ntoso (AKN), for the structured digital publication of African legislation. This standard is being adopted by increasing numbers of African legal information institutes, including those covering Zimbabwe.

Key features:
- XML-based structured legislation
- Hierarchical numbering of provisions
- Metadata including enactment date, commencement date, and amendment history
- Machine-readable format for legal research tools

### Civil Procedure — Common Law SADC Themes

**1. Prescription**
All SADC jurisdictions with a Roman-Dutch law heritage recognise prescription as a defence. The periods vary but the principles of interruption and suspension are broadly similar.

**2. Summary Judgment**
Summary judgment is available in all SADC High Courts where the defendant has entered an appearance but has no bona fide defence. The threshold for defeating summary judgment (raising a defence) is uniformly low.

**3. Provisional Sentence**
The provisional sentence procedure (allowing rapid enforcement of liquid documents like bills of exchange) exists in Zimbabwe and South Africa but not in all SADC jurisdictions.

**4. Interdict (Injunction)**
The requirements for an interdict are essentially the same across all SADC Roman-Dutch jurisdictions: right, injury, and absence of adequate alternative remedy.
