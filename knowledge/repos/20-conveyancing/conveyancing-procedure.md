# Conveyancing Procedure — Zimbabwe and Southern Africa
## Deeds Registries Act [Chapter 20:05] and Practice

### Part 1: Deeds Registry System in Zimbabwe

**Overview**

Zimbabwe operates a system of deeds registration for immovable property. The Deeds Registries Act [Chapter 20:05] and the Deeds Registries Regulations (SI 76 of 2025) govern the registration of all real rights in immovable property.

**Deeds Office**

The Deeds Office is maintained by the Registrar of Deeds. There are Deeds Offices in Harare and Bulawayo. All real rights in immovable property situated in Zimbabwe must be registered in the Deeds Office of the province in which the property is situated.

**Public Register**

The deeds register is a public register. Any person may search the register to ascertain the registered owner and any encumbrances (bonds, servitudes, etc.) affecting a property.

### Part 2: Transfer of Immovable Property

**2.1 Requirements for Transfer**

A transfer of immovable property in Zimbabwe must be effected by a conveyancer (a legal practitioner admitted to practice as a conveyancer) and must be registered in the Deeds Office.

The documents required for transfer include—
(a) a deed of transfer (prepared by the conveyancer);
(b) proof of payment of capital gains tax or exemption;
(c) a rates clearance certificate (for municipal property);
(d) a district land certificate (for rural land);
(e) the original title deed (of the transferor);
(f) identity documents of the parties;
(g) resolution of the transferor if it is a company (COBE Act);
(h) consent of the bondholder if the property is bonded.

**2.2 Lodgement at Deeds Office**

Conveyancing documents are lodged at the Deeds Office in a batch. Documents must be correctly prepared and must comply with all requirements before they will be accepted for lodgement.

**2.3 Examination by Deeds Office**

After lodgement, the documents are examined by the registrar's staff. If defects are found, the documents are returned ("queried") and must be corrected before they can be registered.

**2.4 Registration**

Upon registration—
(a) the transfer of ownership is complete;
(b) the date of registration determines priority between competing rights;
(c) the title deed is printed in the name of the new owner.

### Part 3: Mortgage Bonds

**3.1 Nature of Mortgage Bond**

A mortgage bond is a real security right over immovable property, registered in the Deeds Office. It secures the repayment of a debt by giving the bondholder the right to cause the property to be sold in execution if the debtor defaults.

**3.2 Priorities of Bonds**

Mortgage bonds are ranked in order of registration. A first bond has priority over a subsequent bond. In the event of sale in execution, the proceeds are applied to bonds in order of their priority.

**3.3 Cancellation of Bond**

A mortgage bond may be cancelled when the secured debt is repaid. Cancellation requires—
(a) the bond cancellation documents;
(b) consent of the bondholder;
(c) lodgement at the Deeds Office.

**3.4 Bond Declarations**

The Deeds Registries Regulations (SI 76 of 2025) contain forms of bond declarations which must be annexed to mortgage bond documents. These declarations confirm the terms of the bond, the indebtedness of the mortgagor, and the bondholder's right to sell in execution upon default.

### Part 4: Execution Against Immovable Property

**4.1 Application to Declare Specially Executable**

To execute a judgment against immovable property, the judgment creditor must—
(a) first execute against movables (Rule 227 of the High Court Rules);
(b) if movables are insufficient, apply to the court for an order declaring the immovable property specially executable.

**4.2 Requirements for Special Executability Order**

The application must be—
(a) on notice to the judgment debtor;
(b) on notice to any registered bondholder;
(c) supported by an affidavit showing the judgment debt and insufficiency of movables.

The court conducts a proportionality enquiry: is execution against the immovable property proportionate to the judgment debt?

**4.3 Sheriff's Sale in Execution**

Once an order declaring property specially executable is made, the sheriff of the court—
(a) attaches the property;
(b) advertises the sale in execution in a newspaper;
(c) conducts the sale by public auction;
(d) applies the proceeds to the judgment debt and costs.

The purchaser acquires a clean title, free of the judgment debtor's rights.

### Part 5: Servitudes

**5.1 Types of Servitudes**

Servitudes in Zimbabwe fall into two categories:

**Praedial servitudes** — attached to land and run with the land:
- Right of way (servitus viae)
- Right of drainage (servitus aquae ductus)
- Right of support (servitus oneris ferendi)

**Personal servitudes** — attached to a person:
- Usufruct — the right to use and enjoy property without destroying its substance
- Usus — the right of use (less extensive than usufruct)
- Habitatio — the right to inhabit

**5.2 Registration of Servitudes**

Praedial servitudes are registered in the Deeds Office and appear on the title deed of both the dominant and servient tenements. Unregistered servitudes may exist as contractual rights but cannot bind third parties.

**5.3 Extinction of Servitudes**

Servitudes are extinguished by—
(a) agreement of the parties (followed by Deeds Office cancellation);
(b) merger (the dominant and servient tenements become the same property);
(c) prescription (non-use for 30 years in the case of praedial servitudes);
(d) court order (where the servitude has become impossible or inequitable).

### Part 6: The Deeds Registries Regulations, SI 76 of 2025

**Overview**

The Deeds Registries Regulations, SI 76 of 2025, updated the procedural requirements for lodgement of documents at the Deeds Office, including—
(a) new prescribed forms for transfer deeds, bond documents, and certificates;
(b) updated requirements for identity verification;
(c) electronic lodgement provisions;
(d) revised fee scales.

**Electronic Lodgement**

SI 76 of 2025 introduced provisions for electronic lodgement of certain Deeds Office documents. Conveyancers may now lodge specified documents electronically through the approved system.

**Section 73 Certificates**

In transfers involving deceased estates, a section 73 certificate is required from the Master of the High Court, confirming that the estate has been administered and that the property may be transferred.
